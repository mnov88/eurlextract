English Summary

Facts

ASNEF-EQUIFAX (the controller) is a national credit bureau. The controller included personal data related to a data subject’s debt in their system three times at the request of SD IBERIAN (the creditor). The latter is a company that purchases debt and microloans from banks, and had debt against the data subject. The controller informed the data subject of the inclusion of their data in their credit information system by mail, however, the data subject did not receive the mail because they were sent to the wrong address. The data subject later made three separate requests for the controller to erase the data under Article 17 GDPR. The controller claimed that the requests were forwarded to the creditor, who did not respond. The controller later deleted the information as a precautionary measure and informed the data subject. The data subject learned about the debt being included in the controller's system after consulting ASNEF (National Association of Financial Credit Institutions).

The data subject filed a complaint with the DPA on 15 March 2023, and the DPA began sanctioning proceedings against the controller on 30 May 2024. The data subject argued that the data processing was unlawful, because it was included without verifying the accuracy of the debt or the lawfulness of including it in in the file. Furthermore, the controller did not inform the data subject when the data was included in the file. On the other hand, the controller argued that it had complied with its obligations under the GDPR and national data protection law. This included blocking the data as a precautionary measure and responding to the data subject in accordance with Article 12 GDPR. The controller does not have all the information regarding the debts, and therefore the creditor has the full responsibility of guaranteeing that the debts are lawfully included in the system. Furthermore, the controller argued that it did not process the data for the purposes of a credit information system, and therefore there was no unlawful processing.

Holding

The DPA first established ASNEF-EQUIFAX was a joint controller with the creditor, under Article 20(2) of the national data protection law (LOPDGDD) and Article 26 GDPR. Article 20(2) LOPDGDD explicitly establishes entities maintaining credit information systems and creditors as joint controllers. In addition, ASNEF-EQUIFAX jointly decides the purpose and means of processing with the creditor.

The DPA found a violation of Article 17 GDPR, in conjunction with Article 32 LOPDGDD. Article 20(2) LOPDGDD states that the creditor is responsible for the data being included lawfully, and is responsible for its accuracy. However, the DPA argued that this does not exonerate the controller of its responsibilities. The DPA emphasised that the controller cannot limit itself to forwarding a request for erasure to the creditor or act passively in the absence of a response.[1] The DPA considered that the controller had violated its duty of care; as the manager of the data subject’s file, the controller cannot rely on the decision of a third party to determine how it will act. According to the DPA, the controller had a responsibility to analyse the merits of the request and act in a reasoned manner.[2] Regardless of the arrangement between the controller and the creditor, the data subject had the right to exercise their right to erasure against the controller (Article 26(3) GDPR). The DPA stated that the controller should have blocked the data to prevent its further processing, in accordance with Article 32 LOPDGDD.

The DPA then found a violation of Article 6(1) GDPR. Article 40(5) Royal Decree 1720/2007 (RLOPD)[3] imposes the obligation on the controller to verify with the creditor whether the data subject’s address is accurate. The controller did not do this despite their letters being sent back by the Spanish postal service three times. The obligation to inform the data subject is also present in Article 20 LOPGDDD, which outlines a series of conditions for the processing of data related to credit information systems to be lawful. The DPA stated that the processing of the data was therefore unlawful, as the information obligations under Spanish national law (both the RLOPD and LOPDGDD) were not met. The DPA reiterated that the controller could not act passively and had the duty to actively verify the accuracy of the data subject’s address. Finally, the DPA stated that the decision to block the data did not mean that the previous processing was lawful.

The DPA fined the controller €200,000 in total: €100,000 for the violation of Article 17 GDPR, and €100,000 for the violation of Article 6(1) GDPR. The DPA considered this a serious violation, as the controller had repeatedly refused to verify the data subject’s address. This deprived the data subject of effectively exercising their rights under the GDPR, and was considered negligent behaviour by the controller. In addition, the DPA ordered the controller to continue the cautionary suspension of the data until the requirements for lawful processing are met.

Comment

Explanation for Royal Decree 1720/2007: this law amended the previous national data protection law (LOPD) implementing the EU Data Protection Directive (now derogated by the new national data protection law (LOPDGDD)). Royal Decree 1720/2007 is still in effect as a complement to the GDPR and the LOPDGDD in accordance with national case law (STS 20/12/22, rec. 2737/22): “In the absence of a regulation implementing the new organic law, Royal Decree 1720/2007 serves as the regulatory development of Organic Law 3/2018 [the LOPDGDD], which is necessary for its full effectiveness, without prejudice to the repeal of those provisions of the aforementioned regulation that "contradict, oppose, or are incompatible with the provisions of Regulation (EU) 2016/679 and this organic law".

I. Jurisdiction

In accordance with the provisions of Articles 58.2 and 60 of the GDPR and the provisions of Articles 47, 48.1, 64.2, and 68.1 and 68.2 of the LOPDGDD (Spanish Data Protection Act), the President of the AEPD is competent to resolve this procedure.

Likewise, Article 63.2 of the LOPDGDD establishes that: "The procedures processed by the Spanish Data Protection Agency shall be governed by the provisions of Regulation (EU) 2016/679, by this Organic Law, by the regulatory provisions issued in its development, and, insofar as they do not contradict them, in a subsidiary capacity, by the general rules on administrative procedures."

The respondent states the inconsistency between the Proposed Resolution issued in this sanctioning procedure and the ruling already issued by this AEPD in the previous Resolution of March 7, 2024, issued in the appeal for reconsideration filed by the complainant. The same act is not sanctioned twice, nor is the same aggravating circumstance assessed twice.

Regarding the alleged lack of harm or access to data: the mere registration of data in an ICS without complying with legal requirements, such as valid notification to the data subject, already constitutes unlawful processing punishable under the GDPR. The risk generated by the improper registration itself represents a significant impact on the fundamental right to data protection, and the fact that the complainant, through his own diligence, discovered the inclusion and exercised his rights does not exonerate the respondent from liability or eliminate the effects of the breach.

Regarding the inadmissibility of extending assessments from other procedures: the sanction is based exclusively on the specific facts established in this case, and the individualization of the infringing conduct is perfectly identifiable.

Regarding the violation of Article 6.1 of the GDPR, this Agency does not limit itself to invoking the nature of the activity of the respondent entity in a generic manner. On the contrary, the assessment of negligence as an aggravating circumstance is based on a qualified criterion: the existence of well-established and publicly known jurisprudential principles for years, particularly since the Supreme Court ruling of May 21, 2014, regarding the requirements that must be met to consider the inclusion of personal data in a credit information system lawful. This ruling, which clearly establishes the limits to processing based on the legitimate interest of the creditor, has been repeatedly cited by legal scholars and by resolutions of this same Agency. It is therefore considered that a specialized entity such as Asnef-Equifax, which continuously operates in a sector intensive in the processing of sensitive personal data, cannot claim ignorance of such principles without engaging in particularly significant negligent conduct.

Thus, negligence is not imputed in the abstract, but rather based on the degree of specialization, experience, and duty of enhanced diligence presumed in sector operators, who must be required to meet a higher standard of compliance with personal data protection regulations.

Regarding the violation of Article 17 of the GDPR, it is true that the argument regarding the aggravating circumstance of negligence reproduces the doctrine contained in the Supreme Court ruling of June 5, 2007. However, this doctrine—which precisely distinguishes between the responsibilities of the creditor as data controller and the common file manager as system controller—has also been repeatedly adopted by the Agency in its historical interpretation of the regime applicable to credit information systems.

Therefore, it should be noted that the negligence found is not only based on the reiteration of a structural criterion, but also on the fact that Asnef-Equifax, as joint controller and specialized operator, has been applying an operational procedure that, in the opinion of this Agency, does not meet the requirements arising from the duty to effectively and materially address the exercise of the right to erasure. This is not an automatic or redundant application of aggravating factors, but rather a differentiated assessment based on the specific conduct observed during the procedure. Consequently, the aggravating circumstances applied are sufficiently supported both by the applicable regulations and by established case law, and reflect a considered assessment of the actions of the respondent entity within the framework of its specific legal position and its role in the personal data processing ecosystem. In this context, it cannot be assumed that the standard of due diligence required of an entity such as Asnef-Equifax is comparable to that of any ordinary data controller.
