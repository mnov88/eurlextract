A Court held that a telecom provider lawfully disclosed information about the conclusion of a contract to the credit information agency SCHUFA. The telecom provider could base this disclosure on its legitimate interest to prevent fraud attempts.

English Summary

Facts

A telecom provider (the controller) concluded a contract with a customer (the data subject). Afterwards, the controller forwarded the subject’s data to the credit information agency SCHUFA Holding AG. The disclosure of the data were base on the controller’s interest in preventing fraud.

The data subject brought an action with the Regional Court of Würzburg. He claimed that the disclosure of his data was unlawful. So, he required compensation for non-material damages under Article 82 GDPR, injunctive relief, and a declaration that the controller would be liable for all future material and non-material damages caused by the disclosure.

The Regional Court of Würzburg dismissed the requests. The data subject then appealed the ruling with the Higher Regional Court of Bamberg.

Holding

The Court dismissed the request and held that the controller lawfully disclosed the data to Schufa.

In its analysis of the controller’s legitimate interest, the Court referred to the three requirements laid out by the case law of the Court of Justice[1] and held that they were fulfilled in the case at hand.

First, the Court held that the controller had a real and legitimate interest in avoiding fraud. In this regard, the Court pointed out that many individuals conclude a large number of phone plans in a short time, without paying for those plans, in order to keep the phones that come with them. Credit information agencies such as the controller can help telecom providers avoid such frauds.

Furthermore, the Court pointed out that Recital 47 GDPR lists fraud prevention as an example of a legitimate interest of the data controller.

Then, the Court held that the processing of the data was necessary to prevent fraud. In the Court’s view, it was not apparent that less privacy-invasive means could pursuit the same goal of fraud prevention. In particular, the Court observed that while less invasive fraud prevention techniques were available, they were labor-intensive and not compatible in practice with the large scale of the telecom market.

With regards to the balancing, the Court observed that in the case at hand, the controller disclosed data about successful and timely payments. By its nature, these data (and their disclosure) could not impact the data subject in a negative way. The Court took this into account when balancing the controller’s interest with the data subject’s.

Finally, the Court observed that the data were not sensitive in nature and referred to a very common circumstance about the data subject (that is: the fact that the data subject concluded a contract with a telecom provider).

Opinion

1. The plaintiff's appeal against the judgment of the Regional Court of Würzburg of August 5, 2024, case no. 92 O 2018/23, is dismissed.

2. The plaintiff shall bear the costs of the appeal proceedings.

3. This judgment and the judgment of the Würzburg Regional Court referred to in paragraph 1 are provisionally enforceable without the provision of security.

4. An appeal against this judgment is not allowed.

Reasons for the Decision

1
The plaintiff seeks damages for non-material damages, injunctive relief, and a declaration of the defendant's liability for future material and non-material damages because, after concluding a so-called postpaid mobile phone contract with the plaintiff, the defendant forwarded positive personal data from the contract to the credit agency Schufa Holding AG (hereinafter: Schufa). The Regional Court dismissed the action; with his appeal, the plaintiff continues to pursue his claims from the first instance.

2
Further description of the facts of the case is omitted pursuant to Sections 540 (2) and 313a (1) sentence 1 of the Code of Civil Procedure (ZPO), since neither an appeal against the judgment is admissible, nor can an appeal against non-admission be filed pursuant to Section 544 (2) of the Code of Civil Procedure.

3
The plaintiff's admissible appeal is unsuccessful.

4
1. The plaintiff has no claim for damages against the defendants pursuant to Article 82 (1) of the GDPR, since the (undisputed) transmission of so-called positive data from the mobile phone contract to Schufa by the defendant was justified and therefore does not constitute a violation of the GDPR.

5
It is disputed in case law and literature whether the interests in data transmission asserted by the defendant, namely fraud prevention, over-indebtedness prevention, and enabling default risk forecasts, outweigh the customer's right to informational self-determination and thus the justification under Art. 6 (1) (f) GDPR applies (see, among others: Regional Court of Gießen, GRUR-RS 2024, 7986; Regional Court of Koblenz, GRUR-RS 2024, 14360; Regional Court of Berlin II, GRUR-RS 2024, 16755; Regional Court of Siegen, GRUR-RS 2024, 31639; Regional Court of Kiel, GRUR-RS 2025, 3301; Regional Court of Cologne, GRUR-RS 2025, 1120; Regional Court of Verden, GRUR-RS 2025, 1117; Higher Regional Court of Düsseldorf, GRUR-RS 2024, 32757; Paal, NJW 2024, 1689; against: LG München I, GRUR-RS 2023, 10317; LG Köln, GRUR-RS 2023, 9811).

6
The Senate agrees with the majority opinion that the processing carried out with the transfer of the data (Article 4 No. 2 GDPR) was justified under Article 6 (1) (f) GDPR.

7
a) According to the case law of the ECJ (judgment of July 4, 2023, case C-252/21, NJW 2023, 2997, paras. 105 et seq.), the processing of personal data pursuant to Art. 6 (1) (f) GDPR is lawful under three cumulative conditions: first, a legitimate interest must be pursued by the controller or a third party; second, the processing of personal data must be necessary to achieve that legitimate interest; and third, the interests or fundamental rights and freedoms of the data subject must not override the legitimate interest of the controller or a third party. In this regard, according to Art. 13 (d) GDPR, the controller is obliged to inform the data subject of the legitimate interests pursued at the time personal data is collected from them if such processing is based on Art. 6 (1) (f) GDPR. Furthermore, the legitimate interest in processing the data must not be reasonably achieved just as effectively by other means that less interfere with the fundamental rights and freedoms of the data subjects, in particular the rights to respect for private life and to the protection of personal data guaranteed by Art. 7 and 8 of the European Charter of Fundamental Rights. Furthermore, the interests or fundamental freedoms and rights of the person whose data is to be protected must not outweigh the legitimate interest of the controller or a third party; this requires a balancing of the respective conflicting rights and interests, the outcome of which fundamentally depends on the specific circumstances of the individual case.

8
b) These conditions for a lawful data transfer are met here.

9
aa) A legitimate interest within the meaning of Art. 6 (1) (f) GDPR exists here, in any case, with the aim of fraud prevention.

10
aaa) The provision does not explicitly define what constitutes a legitimate interest. However, Recital 47 of the GDPR provides guidance. The term is to be understood broadly and encompasses any interest recognized by the legal system. This excludes only those interests that are rejected by the legal system. Recognized interests include not only legal interests, but also actual, economic, or idealistic interests. Mere public interests, in contrast, are insufficient. Fraud prevention, in particular, is explicitly mentioned in Recital 47. Legitimate interests may also arise from the right to make a profit (Art. 16 of the European Charter of Fundamental Rights), including the preparation of transactions (such as credit checks), and the defense of one's own assets (Art. 17 of the European Charter of Fundamental Rights). A legitimate interest may not only be that of the controller itself, but also that of a third party (Article 4 No. 10 GDPR), which the controller wishes to promote, but not an interest of the data subject (LG Siegen judgment of October 21, 2024 – 8 O 43/24, GRUR-RS 2024, 31639, para. 46).

11
bbb) Accordingly, fraud prevention is readily recognized as a legitimate interest. The plaintiff also did not challenge the defendant's argument that in cases where potential customers conclude an inexplicably large number of mobile phone contracts in a short period of time, the customer's intention can be inferred to be to acquire the expensive hardware, and that the credit agencies have developed more detailed evaluation methods for this purpose (Higher Regional Court of Düsseldorf, judgment of October 31, 2024 – I-20 U 51/24, GRUR-RS 2024, 32757, para. 44).

12
bb) It is undisputed that at the start of the contract, the defendant also informed the plaintiff that positive data would be transmitted to Schufa for the purpose of profiling (Exhibit B2).

13
cc) A milder measure is not apparent. Thus, the transmission of so-called positive data regarding the conclusion of contracts is generally considered to be significantly less harmful than stigmatizing negative data. This is also true because the absence of positive data as factors that favor the score value can lead to a “negative bias” (LG Kiel judgment of 21 February 2025 – 4 O 100/24, GRUR-RS 2025, 3301 paras. 21-24; Paal, loc. cit., para. 19). To the extent that some argue that fraud prevention can also be achieved through milder measures, such as more labor-intensive acquisition with higher control thresholds or through modified service concepts (Munich Regional Court I, judgment of April 25, 2023 – 33 O 5976/22, ZD 2024, 46), these measures do not do justice to the highly automated mass business of telecommunications service providers and, as a result, may be a milder, but not appropriate, means of achieving the legitimate interest (Giessen Regional Court judgment of April 3, 2024 – 9 O 523/23, GRUR-RS 2024, 7986, para. 17).

14
dd) In conclusion, the interests or fundamental freedoms and rights of the plaintiff do not outweigh the legitimate interests of third parties described above. In this respect, it had to be taken into account that the transmitted positive data did not constitute sensitive data, but rather data that applies to a not insignificant portion of the population and which ultimately merely conveys the information that person XY has concluded a postpaid mobile phone contract. It is also not apparent that the transmitted data could have any negative impact on the plaintiff; in particular, there is no reason to fear a negative impact on his creditworthiness. Rather, as explained above, the presence of positive data can have a positive influence on the creditworthiness score. To the extent that the plaintiff argues that the subsequent deletion of positive data could have a negative impact on the score, this is indeed true. However, it is not understandable why the plaintiff's score should ultimately be worse than it would have been without the prior transmission. Furthermore, according to the defendant's unchallenged statement, Schufa's contractual partners are not provided with the plaintiff's positive data, which is the subject of this dispute, upon corresponding inquiries, but only with the score calculated from it. Furthermore, it should be considered that fraud prevention does not initially directly benefit the plaintiff personally. However, the question of creditworthiness is of particular importance to all market participants for their own pricing, as greater uncertainty must be redistributed through higher prices. In this respect, an indirect benefit from the report can at least be established for the plaintiff as well (LG Kiel, ibid., para. 24).

15
Overall, the lawfulness of the data processing can therefore be assumed.

16
2. Contrary to the opinion of the Regional Court, the plaintiff's application for an injunction is already inadmissible because it does not satisfy the requirement of specificity under Section 253 (2) No. 2 of the Code of Civil Procedure.

17
The application does not describe in sufficient detail which of the plaintiff's data the defendant should refrain from transmitting. The supposedly unambiguous definition of "the plaintiff's positive data" as "personal data that does not contain payment histories or other non-contractual conduct, but rather information about the commissioning, execution, and termination of a contract" still contains vague legal terms or formulations that require interpretation (payment histories; non-contractual conduct; commissioning, execution, and termination of a contract), the interpretation of which would be left to the enforcement court, which is inadmissible. For this reason alone, the injunction lacks the necessary specificity, rendering it inadmissible. (Higher Regional Court of Koblenz, advisory decision of February 5, 2025 – 5 U 1033/24, GRUR-RS 2025, 1765, paras. 25, 26).

18
3. The plaintiff is also not entitled to the requested findings.

19
It can remain undecided whether the plaintiff has conclusively asserted the interest in obtaining a declaratory judgment required under Section 256 (1) of the Code of Civil Procedure. This is a genuine procedural prerequisite only for a judgment granting the judgment. If, however, the action is unfounded – as is the case here – it can be dismissed on substantive grounds, regardless of any existing interest in obtaining a declaratory judgment (Federal Court of Justice, judgment of October 10, 2017 – XI ZR 456/16, NJW 2018, 227, para. 16; Zöller-Greger, Code of Civil Procedure, 34th ed., § 256, para. 13).

20
The decision on costs is based on Section 97 (1) of the Code of Civil Procedure. The ruling on provisional enforceability follows from Sections 708 No. 10, 711, and 713 of the Code of Civil Procedure.

21
The conduct of a preliminary ruling procedure under Article 267 TFEU to clarify one of the examined and established aspects is not required. At least to the extent relevant to the decision, the interpretation of the relevant EU law concepts has been clarified by the case law of the ECJ.

22
The appeal was not admissible. The decision does not contradict decisions of other higher regional courts. The Senate also followed the case law of the ECJ without deviating from it. The legal dispute is also of no fundamental importance.
